## 👋 In one paragraph

I am a backend engineer who has spent the better part of a decade writing services that other people quietly depend on. Hospitality procurement systems for 20000+ properties. Credit-reporting platforms that decide whether someone gets a loan. Payment-gateway simulators for a defence-grade vendor. Open Finance infrastructure for a regional bank. The industries change, the satisfying part does not: a quiet system, a clean event log, a runbook nobody had to open this quarter.

I am, in fairness, not the developer you want if you need a beautifully animated landing page. I am the developer you want if your webhook fired three times for the same payment and your finance team has questions.

## 💭 Things I have come to believe, slowly and at some cost

> **1.** Idempotency keys are not optional in any payment system, ever. This is the hill. I will die on it. I have, in fact, already been buried on it twice and got back up both times to insist on it again.
>
> **2.** Most *microservices problems* are distributed-transaction problems wearing a costume. The costume is usually convincing. The transaction is still there.
>
> **3.** A monorepo is fine. A polyrepo is fine. The shape of your CI pipeline is what is actually killing you.
>
> **4.** If a service has no consumer-driven contract test, it does not have an API. It has a suggestion.
>
> **5.** Commit messages, README files, and changelogs are engineering deliverables. The person on call at 2 a.m. is reading them. Behave accordingly.
>
> **6.** Postgres covers more ground than the architecture diagram usually admits. This is not a controversial opinion; it is an unfashionable one.
>
> **7.** Mentoring is not a side activity. It is the multiplier. The juniors I worked with at LMD are senior engineers now, and that is a more durable line on the résumé than any feature I shipped that quarter.
